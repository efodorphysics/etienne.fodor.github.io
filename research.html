Active fluctuations in living matter


Living systems operate far from equilibrium due to the continuous injection of energy provided by ATP supply. The dynamics of the intracellular components is driven by both thermal equilibrium fluctuations, and active stochastic forces generated by the molecular motors. Tracer particles are injected in living cells to study these fluctuations [1]. Alternatively, vesicles which are already present in the cytoplasm serve as probes of the intracellular dynamics.

To sort out genuine nonequilibrium fluctuations from purely thermal effects, we combine passive and active microrheology methods. They consist in measuring the spontaneous tracer fluctuations, and extracting the response from an external oscillatory perturbation, respectively [2]. By testing the fluctuation-dissipation theorem, we quantify the deviation from equilibrium appearing at low frequency. Removing the thermal contribution in the tracer fluctuations, we estimate the spectrum of the active forces.

We recapitulate theoretically the observed fluctuations by modeling the dynamics with a confining harmonic potential which experiences random bursts as a result of motor activity [3]. This minimal model allows us to quantify the time scales of the active forces, along with the length scale of the ensuing fluctuations [4,5]. Finally, we estimate the energy injected by the activity and the one dissipated in the surrounding environment, leading us to define an efficiency of the energy conversion driving the tracer's dynamics [6].

Furthermore, we investigate the fluctuations in epithelial tissues by tracking tricellular junctions. These are involved in the exchanges between neighbouring cells, thus serving as hallmark of the tissue dynamics. Specifically, we highlight the non-Gaussian statistics of the junction displacement as a result of directed motion events. Describing the dynamics as an effective caging, we demonstrate that our prediction is consistent with the full statistics of the junctions. It allows us to reveal a systematic decrease of diffusion coefficient, persistence time and persistence length as motor activity is gradually inhibited [7].

Nonequilibrium properties of self-propelled particles


Active particles are able to extract energy from their environment to perform a self-propelled motion. The permanent energy conversion at the basis of such motion gives rise to a steady energy flux in the system. In that respect, an assembly of self-propelled particles is commonly regarded as a nonequilibrium system.

Yet, many active systems share common features with equilibrium ones. The sedimentation of active colloids is well described by using equilibrium concepts, in line with experimental observations [1]. Moreover, a phase separation in athermal systems known as motility-induced phase separation (MIPS) has been reported for interacting active particles [2]. Recent works have shown that the characterization of phase equilibria in active systems shares similarities with an equilibrium process [3].

Our goal lies in determining to which extent active matter can be described as a nonequilibrium system. To this aim, we consider a model system of active matter which has recently received a lot of attention. It consists in interacting particles performing a persistent random walk. This directed motion is distinct from run-and-tumble since the self-propulsion is now allowed to fluctuate [4-6].

New analytic developments are required, beyond the common tools of equilibrium physics, to capture the deviation from equilibrium. The breakdown of the time-reversal symmetry, arising from the active component of the dynamics, is quantified through the rate of entropy production. The fluctuation-dissipation theorem, valid for an equilibrium process, can be extended out-of-equilibrium. Finally, the non-Boltzmann measure can explain for the phase separation appearing under strictly repulsive interactions, as reported numerically [7].
